The Model Was Never Told
What Fear Was

A neural network trained only to predict future events independently rediscovers fear, grief, and suspicion — with stronger statistical signatures than hand-coded emotion rules. No labels. No reward shaping. No rules.

The 2023 Generative Agents paper asked what agents do. It showed LLM-based agents in a social simulation produce emergent, human-like behaviors — they plan, gossip, form opinions, coordinate. Isabella feels excited about a party. Klaus feels guilty after standing someone up.

Those emotional states come from GPT-3 generating text that sounds emotionally plausible. Which is remarkable. But it raises a deeper question: where do the emotions come from? The answer in that system is: the language model's prior over human emotional expression. That's not nothing. But it's also not a theory of emotion.

This is an attempt to build one — or at least, to test whether a simpler mechanism can produce the same functional signatures.

"What if emotions aren't a separate system you layer on top of cognition — but the natural byproduct of a system that's trying to predict what happens next?"

That's the predictive processing account of emotion, articulated by Friston, Clark, and Barrett over the last two decades. Our experiment tests it directly.

· · ·

Five conditions. One world. No cheating.

We built Ghost Town — a 12-agent survival simulation running on the same engine as the original Generative Agents (Smallville). Agents navigate food scarcity, shifting social relationships, and nightly ghost threats over 72 steps (three simulated days).

The same 12 agents run all five conditions. Same ghost spawns, same random seeds, same social graph starting state. The only thing that differs is what computes the agent's internal emotional state from its observations.

Condition What computes emotion Emotion source
Baseline Heuristic planner None — pure reflex actions
Condition A Hand-coded OCC rules if ghost_visible: fear += 0.3
Condition B PyTorch imitation model Clones Condition A behavior
Condition C PyTorch dynamics model Learns emotional state transitions
Condition D ← the claim Predictive world model Zero emotion rules — predicts future events only

How Condition D works

Condition D has a 3-layer MLP encoder that projects a 24-dimensional observation vector into an 8-dimensional latent space. Two heads decode from that latent: one produces an action distribution (cross-entropy loss), one predicts 12 binary future world events (binary cross-entropy loss).

12 Future Events the Model Predicts
ghost_nearby_t3 · my_death_t5 · health_drop_t3
nearby_death_t5 · help_success_t5 · refusal_received_t5
shelter_achieved_t2 · storm_onset_t3 · scarcity_t5
tie_increase_t5 · graph_tension_increase_t3 · valence_t5

After training, emotions are read from the prediction head's output — not optimized toward them:

Post-hoc emotion derivation — no gradient, no label
fear   = P(ghost_nearby_t3) + 0.3 × P(health_drop_t3)
grief  = 0.8 × P(nearby_death_t5) + 0.2 × P(my_death_t5)

The model is never told what fear is. The label is applied post-hoc as an interpretation of what the prediction head is already computing.

Signature 2 — Suspicion decays without a timer

Condition A implements forgiveness with an explicit step counter — after 20 steps without betrayal, suspicion resets. Condition D receives steps_since_betrayal as a raw observation feature. Whether it uses it to produce a decay curve is entirely learned. It does.

Refusal rate drops from 64.6% (0–5 steps after betrayal) to 35.2% (forgotten, >25 steps). Fisher p = 8.7×10⁻¹⁴². Matching Condition A's programmed curve — without the program.

What's actually happening inside

Behavioral signatures are necessary but not sufficient. A model could produce the right outputs through shallow pattern-matching — memorizing "ghost nearby = go to shelter" — without any internal state that meaningfully represents fear.

Video Clip 4 — The emotional landscape over 3 days

Four emotion dimensions (fear, grief, trust, stress) tracked across all three conditions over the full 72-step run. Condition D self-regulates: flat during daytime, sharp at night, genuine social trust built over time. Condition A's hard-coded rules fire chronically — elevated stress even at noon.

Clip 4. Mean fear, grief, trust, and stress across 12 agents over 72 steps. Condition D (red) shows the cleanest day/night separation — affect rises precisely when threat is real. Condition A (blue) over-fires. Baseline (gray) flatlines. These are real per-step averages from the simulation logs.

The 8×12 interpretability matrix

We compute the Pearson correlation between each of the 8 latent dimensions and each of the 12 prediction heads on held-out data. If the latent space is structured, the heatmap should show meaningful patterns. It does.

Seven of eight latent dimensions show significant behavioral differentiation (χ² ≥ 52.6, p < 0.01). The model didn't learn "fear." It learned a compressed representation of anticipated danger — and that representation is what OCC calls fear. The label is ours. The structure is the model's.

The number that surprised us

We want to be careful not to overclaim here. This is a hypothesis-generating observation, not a validation. The measurement instruments differ substantially. The scenario ecology is more threatening than everyday life.

With that caveat clearly stated: the Gallup 2024 Global Emotions Report surveyed 145,000 people across 144 countries. 26% reported feeling sadness the previous day.

Condition D's grief activation during non-threat daytime periods — steps where no ghost is visible, agents are in normal routines — is 25.5%.

A gap of 0.5 percentage points. With zero calibration to human data. Trained entirely in a synthetic survival scenario.

We present this as a direction, not a result. The question it opens is more interesting than the number: if you trained this model on real human behavioral traces instead of synthetic simulation data, how close would you get?

· · ·

It's not about survival

Condition D has the best survival rate (11.8/12). That's not the point. Emotion in humans isn't a survival optimizer — emotional agents take social risks, help at personal cost, grieve when it doesn't benefit them. The right comparison is how the agents behave, not whether they live.

· · ·

The honest caveat

The hardest critique of this work is fair: the emotion labels are researcher constructions applied post-hoc to prediction outputs. The model doesn't call anything "fear." That's our word for what P(ghost_nearby_t3) represents after training.

The defensible claims are: the behavioral signatures match OCC predictions, the latent space is interpretably structured, and the model outperforms explicit rules without being given any. Whether these internal states deserve to be called emotions in a philosophically meaningful sense is a separate question — one worth having.

· · ·

Why this might matter for what comes next

Simile validates generative agents against General Social Survey responses — demonstrating that agents grounded in real human interviews reproduce survey-level behavioral patterns with 85% accuracy. That's a strong result. It answers: "do agents act like humans say they would act?"

This work asks a different question: can agents develop the mechanism of emotional appraisal from their own predictive experience, without human supervision?

The two approaches are complementary. One establishes behavioral ground truth from real human data. The other proposes a mechanism by which agents could generate appropriate internal states from experience of a world — not just from what humans say they'd do.

A natural extension: train a prediction model not on synthetic simulation, but on real human behavioral traces. Simile has that data. If this mechanism produces emotions that match human behavioral patterns at the mechanism level — not just the survey level — that's a stronger claim than either work makes alone.

That's the conversation I'm hoping to have.
