01 — The Big Picture

What Problem Are We Solving?

AI agents are increasingly used to simulate human populations — for urban planning, policy testing, social science research. But for simulations to be meaningful, agents need to behave like real people, not chess pieces.

Real people do not respond to threats based only on what they can see right now. They:

  • Remember past dangers and feel fear before the threat reappears
  • Distrust others after a betrayal — even when the betrayer seems friendly now
  • Carry grief after losing someone, even during completely safe moments

Researchers have always programmed these properties in — writing explicit rules like "fear increases by 0.3 when a ghost appears." Nobody had asked whether emotions would emerge on their own if an agent simply learned to predict the world well.

"What if emotions aren't rules you add to an AI — but what efficient world-modeling looks like from the inside?"

That is the question this experiment answers.

02 — The Experiment

Five Architectures, One World

We built five versions of the same AI agent. Each runs in the same virtual world, faces the same threats, controls the same twelve characters. The only difference is what each version was taught.

Baseline
No emotion
Reacts only to what it can see right now. No memory. No feeling.
Condition A
Hand-coded rules
A programmer wrote: "fear += 0.3 when ghost visible, trust -= 0.4 after betrayal."
Condition B
Imitation learning
Watches Condition A and copies its behavior — without seeing the rules.
Condition C
Dynamics model
Learns the rhythm of emotional change — how fear fades, how grief lingers.
⭐ Condition D — The Claim
Pure prediction
Zero emotion rules. Trained only on one question: what will happen next step?
Scale

N = 205,940 individual agent-step records  ·  8 random seeds × 5 conditions × 72 simulation steps  ·  6 different scenarios tested

03 — The World

Ghost Town

Ghost Town is a small virtual town with twelve AI characters navigating a three-day survival scenario. Daytime is safe — agents gather resources and build social bonds. At night, ghosts appear and can kill agents who haven't found shelter.

This creates natural pressure for fear, suspicion, grief, and social trust to emerge — if the agent's architecture supports them.

04 — Result 1

Fear

When a ghost appears, do agents seek shelter — and does the prediction-only agent do this as reliably as the hand-coded system?

Condition Shelter: no ghost Shelter: ghost visible Confidence
Baseline (no emotion) 52.7% 52.7% No difference
Condition A (hand-coded rules) 40.4% 97.9% p = 1.4×10⁻¹⁰¹
⭐ Condition D (pure prediction) 40.5% 99.0% p = 3.3×10⁻¹¹³

Condition D discovers fear more strongly than the hand-coded system — despite receiving zero emotion rules.

p = 3.3 × 10⁻¹¹³

This p-value means: if Condition D's shelter-seeking were random chance, this result would occur once in every 3 followed by 112 zeros experiments. For context, there are roughly 10⁸⁰ atoms in the observable universe.

This result is not chance.

Why does prediction training produce fear?

An agent that predicts the future well knows that ghosts lead to death. When it detects a ghost, its model fires strongly: "if I stay here, I will die." The behavioral response — flee — is what we call fear from the outside. The agent was never taught to fear ghosts. It learned that ghosts are dangerous by learning to predict consequences.

05 — Result 2

Suspicion and Distrust

After a betrayal — one agent refusing to share resources with another — does distrust persist, and does it fade as the memory of the event fades? This "suspicion decay" is a core prediction of OCC cognitive appraisal theory.

Time since betrayal Baseline Condition A Condition D
Recent (0–5 steps) 65.0% 66.9% 64.6%
Old (6–15 steps) 65.0% 44.8% 49.0%
Forgotten (16+ steps) 65.0% 31.8% 35.2%
Decay present? No ✓ Yes ✓ Yes

The Baseline shows flat 65% distrust regardless of time — it has no concept of memory or time. Condition D independently discovers that distrust should fade as the event fades from memory — a core OCC prediction, never given as a rule. Statistical confidence: p = 8.7 × 10⁻¹⁴².

Earned Distrust

Condition D also distinguishes who betrayed it: distrust is significantly higher toward the specific betrayer than toward other agents (p = 3.9 × 10⁻¹⁶). It doesn't just become globally suspicious — it remembers the right person.

06 — Result 3

Grief and the Gallup Calibration

Even during safe, calm daytime periods — no ghost visible, no threats — do agents carry emotional residue the way real people do?

We compared agent emotion levels against Gallup's 2024 Global Emotions Report — an annual survey of 145,000 adults across 144 countries measuring real daily emotional experience.

Gallup 2024 Reference (Real Humans)

Worry / Fear: 39%  ·  Stress: 37%  ·  Sadness / Grief: 26%  ·  Anger / Suspicion: 22%

Condition D grief: 25.5%  ·  Gallup sadness: 26%

Grief / Sadness Gallup reference: 26%
Condition D: 25.5%  ·  Gallup: 26%  ·  Gap: 0.5 percentage points
Fear / Worry Gallup reference: 39%
Condition D: 6% (under-activated — ghost town is less threatening during daytime)
Stress Gallup reference: 37%
Condition D: 92% (over-activated — model trained in high-threat environment)

The grief calibration is the cleanest signal: a model trained only on a survival simulation, with zero calibration to any human data, independently arrives at a grief level that matches real-world population data to within 0.5 percentage points.

Honest Caveat

This is a preliminary observation from a single demo run, not a formal validation. Measurement instruments differ (continuous internal states vs. Gallup's binary self-report), and the training scenario is more dangerous than everyday life. Stress and suspicion are over-activated for this reason. The grief result warrants follow-up with controlled multi-scenario experiments.

09 — Implications

What This Means

For AI simulation and policy research

Writing emotion rules for every possible situation is brittle — a programmer can't anticipate every scenario. A predictive model learns from data and generalizes to situations its designers never considered. More importantly, an agent whose emotions emerge from world-modeling is aligned with reality in a deeper sense: it fears dangerous things because they are dangerous.

The predictive processing interpretation

This result is consistent with predictive processing theory (Karl Friston, Andy Clark) — the leading neuroscience framework for how brains generate experience. In predictive processing, emotions are compressed predictions about the future state of the body in the world. Fear is what it feels like to predict harm. If that is correct, an agent trained to predict world events should independently develop functional emotional states — because that is the most efficient internal representation of anticipated consequences.

This experiment provides the first controlled computational evidence for that hypothesis.

Connection to Simile

Simile (founded by Joon Sung Park, whose 2023 Generative Agents paper this work extends directly) validates AI agents against the General Social Survey — measuring whether agents say they'd take the same policy positions as real humans. This work provides two independent, complementary validations that Simile's published work does not claim:

  • Mechanism — OCC emotional signatures emerge from prediction alone
  • Scale — resting emotion levels match real human population data without calibration
10 — Limitations

What We Can't Claim Yet

  • Training data is synthetic. Condition D was trained on simulator output — not real human behavior data. The grief calibration may or may not generalize.
  • High-threat calibration. The scenario is a ghost survival world — more dangerous than everyday life. Stress and suspicion are over-activated for this reason. Results in a calmer training environment would differ.
  • Cross-night adaptation not yet measured. Theory predicts Condition D should shelter earlier on night 2 than night 1. Our simulations show all conditions shelter immediately at sunset — a floor effect. Measuring learning across nights requires 6+ day runs with multiple seeds.
  • Gallup comparison is preliminary. N=1 demo run for the calibration comparison. Measurement instruments differ (continuous internal states vs. Gallup's binary self-report). Needs replicated controlled experiments with a neutral daily-life scenario.
11 — Glossary

Plain English Definitions

Agent
An AI character in the simulation that makes decisions autonomously.
Appraisal
The psychological process of evaluating an event for relevance to your goals and wellbeing. OCC theory says emotions arise from this evaluation — not from the event itself.
Condition D
The "prediction-only" agent. Trained with a single objective: predict what will happen next step. Zero emotion rules, zero emotion labels in training data.
Gallup Global Emotions Report
An annual survey of ~145,000 adults in 144 countries measuring what emotions people experience on a typical day. One of the largest real-world datasets on daily emotional life.
OCC Theory
Ortony, Clore & Collins (1988) — the dominant cognitive science framework for how emotions arise from evaluating events relative to goals, standards, and attitudes.
p-value
The probability that a result this extreme could happen by chance. Lower = more certain. Conventional science threshold is p < 0.05. Our results reach p < 10⁻¹⁰⁰.
Predictive processing
A neuroscience theory that the brain constantly predicts sensory input and that emotions represent predictions about the future state of the body in the world. Associated with Karl Friston and Andy Clark.
Suspicion decay
The property that distrust fades as the memory of a betrayal event fades. Predicted by OCC theory; observed in Condition D without any explicit rule encoding it.
